## Reviewer 1: Methodology Skeptic

**Expertise:** Multi-agent systems, cost optimization, LLM routing

### Scores

| Criterion    | Score |
|-------------|-------|
| Quality      | 5     |
| Clarity      | 5     |
| Originality  | 7     |
| Significance | 6     |

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject

### Strengths

1. **Clean cost algebra formulation.** The decomposition of topology cost into structural parameters (N, D, R) is elegant and practically useful. The formulas C_flat = c_base, C_hier = c_base * N * D, C_debate = c_base * N^2 * R are intuitive and the paper correctly identifies that these multipliers are backbone-independent.

2. **Well-designed ablation study.** The three ablations (remove difficulty estimator, remove quality threshold, remove cost model) cleanly isolate each component's contribution. The finding that removing the difficulty estimator drops accuracy to 48.2% while removing the cost model only shrinks savings to 19% is informative and non-obvious.

3. **Honest assessment of the accuracy-cost tradeoff.** The paper does not hide the 1.5pp accuracy drop. The discussion of when this tradeoff is and is not acceptable (production vs. safety-critical) is mature.

### Weaknesses

1. **Critical backbone inconsistency.** The paper contradicts itself on which LLM was used. Section 4.5 (experiments.tex line 65) states "All experiments use Claude Opus 4.6 as the backbone LLM." Table 1's caption (results.tex line 9) states "All topologies use GPT-4o backbone." The Discussion limitations section (discussion.tex line 21) again says "all experiments use a single backbone (GPT-4o)." This is not a cosmetic issue -- it calls into question which results correspond to which backbone, and whether the cost algebra's R^2 > 0.95 validation was conducted on the stated model.

2. **Insufficient evaluation scale.** The test set contains only 22 tasks. CostRouter's 63.1% accuracy at this scale means roughly 14 correct out of 22. A single routing error flips the accuracy by 4.5 percentage points. The paper acknowledges single-run evaluation but still makes strong claims ("42% cost reduction") that have unknown variance at n=22. The FrugalGPT-adapted and BAMAS-adapted baselines are defined in Section 4.3 but their results are absent from Table 1, making the comparison incomplete.

3. **Cost algebra validation is near-tautological.** The R^2 > 0.95 claim for the cost algebra fitting actual token counts is tested across only 3 topology families whose cost structures are directly parameterized by the formulas. Fitting 3-parameter polynomials to data generated by 3 structural categories is not a stringent test. A more rigorous validation would test on topology variants not used in derivation (e.g., sparse debate, agent dropout configurations, or mixed hierarchical-debate hybrids).

### Detailed Comments

- The D-I-T difficulty estimator is trained on 282 examples (82 real + 200 synthetic paraphrases). The synthetic augmentation strategy is underspecified: how were paraphrases generated? If by LLM, the D-I-T preservation claim needs validation. If by rule-based methods, the diversity is limited.
- Theorem 1's bound is admitted to be loose (excess cost up to 472 * c_base vs oracle total of 400-600 * c_base). A bound that permits 80-118% excess cost over oracle is not practically informative.
- The adapted baselines (FrugalGPT-adapted, BAMAS-adapted) are author reimplementations that change the routing dimension from model to topology. Without the original authors' validation, these adaptations may not faithfully represent the baseline systems' capabilities.
- The Pareto frontier description is inconsistent: Section 3.4 says the knee is at tau=0.85, while Section 5.4 says the sweet spot is 0.5-0.7 with the operating point at tau=0.6. These cannot both be correct.

## Reviewer 2: Novelty Assessor

**Expertise:** LLM cost optimization, FrugalGPT, RouteLLM, agent orchestration

### Scores

| Criterion    | Score |
|-------------|-------|
| Quality      | 6     |
| Clarity      | 7     |
| Originality  | 7     |
| Significance | 6     |

**Recommendation:** Borderline (Weak Accept / Weak Reject)

### Strengths

1. **Genuinely novel axis of optimization.** The paper's core insight -- that topology is a bigger cost lever than model choice (24x vs 2-3x) -- is well-argued and, to my knowledge, not previously formalized. The explicit framing of model routing (FrugalGPT) and topology routing (CostRouter) as orthogonal, composable axes is a real contribution to how the community thinks about cost optimization.

2. **Thorough related work positioning.** Section 2 covers 25+ systems across model routing, topology search, cost-constrained agents, and difficulty estimation. The paper clearly articulates why each prior system does not solve the topology routing problem: FrugalGPT optimizes model choice within fixed structure, BAMAS optimizes offline, AgentConductor is code-generation specific, etc. The gap is well-motivated.

3. **Practical design choices.** The router uses zero LLM calls for routing (MLP classifier + pre-computed profiles), runs in <2ms, and drops in as middleware. This is not a research prototype that requires fine-tuning or RL training. The engineering pragmatism increases the chance of real-world adoption.

### Weaknesses

1. **The cost algebra is intellectually lightweight.** The formulas (flat=1x, hierarchical=N*D, debate=N^2*R) are useful rules of thumb but not a deep theoretical contribution. Any practitioner who has debugged a multi-agent system would arrive at similar estimates. The "algebra" framing overpromises relative to what is essentially dimensional analysis of token flow patterns.

2. **CostRouter is architecturally straightforward.** The system is a two-stage filter: predict accuracy per topology, pick cheapest above threshold. This is the natural extension of FrugalGPT's cascade logic to a parallel selection setting. The difficulty estimator (MLP on lexical/structural features) and threshold selection (sweep Pareto frontier) are standard techniques. The novelty is in the problem formulation, not the solution mechanism.

3. **Empirical validation is too thin for a top venue.** 82 tasks, single run, 22-task test set, one backbone (with inconsistent naming). ICML expects rigorous evaluation: multiple seeds, statistical significance, scaling behavior, transfer to new domains. The paper reads as a strong position paper with preliminary experiments rather than a complete empirical study. The missing FrugalGPT-adapted and BAMAS-adapted results from Table 1 further weaken the empirical story.

### Assessment vs. FrugalGPT/RouteLLM

The paper correctly identifies that it operates on an orthogonal axis. FrugalGPT routes across models (2-3x cost range); CostRouter routes across topologies (1-36x cost range). This is a legitimate distinction. However, FrugalGPT and RouteLLM both demonstrated their routing on thousands of queries with multiple model pairs and confidence intervals. CostRouter's 22-task evaluation does not meet the same evidentiary standard.

**Is this ICML caliber?** Not in current form. The framing is ICML-worthy. The validation is workshop-level. With 500+ tasks, multiple backbones, joint model-topology routing experiments, and statistical rigor, this could be a strong submission.

**Expertise:** Scientific writing, reproducibility, publication standards

| Criterion    | Score |
|-------------|-------|

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject

### Strengths

1. **Exceptionally clear prose.** The writing is direct, avoids jargon inflation, and explains technical choices with reasoning rather than assertion. The introduction's "But what if the execution structure is the bigger lever?" is an effective rhetorical hook. The limitations section ("We count five, and they matter") is refreshingly honest.

2. **Zero AI-pattern flags.** No instances of "delve," "leverage," "utilize," "it is worth noting," "comprehensive," "robust," "cutting-edge," "groundbreaking," "innovative," "aforementioned," "paradigm shift," "noteworthy," "landscape," "multifaceted," "pivotal," "underscore," "realm," or "facilitate." The writing reads as human-authored throughout.

3. **Well-structured argument flow.** The paper follows a clean logical arc: motivate the problem (Section 1), position against prior work (Section 2), formalize the solution (Section 3), describe experiments (Section 4), report results (Section 5), discuss limitations (Section 6), conclude (Section 7). Each section builds on the previous one without redundancy.

### Weaknesses

1. **Figure placeholder renders as literal text.** methods.tex line 63 contains `{[}FIGURE: CostRouter architecture diagram...{]}` which renders as visible plaintext in the compiled PDF. This is the architecture diagram -- arguably the most important figure for understanding the system. Its absence as a proper LaTeX figure is a submission-blocking defect.

2. **Backbone model inconsistency is a clarity failure.** Three different claims about the backbone appear: "Claude Opus 4.6" (experiments), "GPT-4o" (Table 1 caption, Discussion), and both mentioned together (Introduction). A reader cannot determine which backbone produced the reported numbers. This undermines the credibility of every quantitative claim in the paper.

3. **Table 1 is incomplete.** Section 4.3 defines six baselines (always-flat, always-hierarchical, always-debate, FrugalGPT-adapted, BAMAS-adapted, oracle). Table 1 reports only five rows, omitting FrugalGPT-adapted and BAMAS-adapted. These are the most interesting comparisons since they represent existing routing approaches adapted to the topology setting. Their absence from the main results table is a significant gap.

### Additional Clarity Issues

- **Pareto knee inconsistency:** Section 3.4 states the knee is "around tau=0.85." Section 5.4 says the "sweet spot sits between 0.5 and 0.7." These are contradictory. The operating point tau=0.6 is far below the stated knee of 0.85.
- **Cross-reference breakage:** Prior submission check identified 3 broken \ref{} commands (sec:discussion, sec:limitations, sec:results) that render as "??" in the PDF.
- **Section 5.1 reference mismatch:** The methods section (3.1) references "Section 5.1" for R^2 validation of the cost algebra, but Section 5.1 in results is titled "Main Comparison" and does not contain a dedicated cost algebra validation subsection.
- **Some citations feel tangential:** \cite{wu_2023} after "CostRouter threads the needle" (results.tex line 24) cites AutoGen, which is not about cost threading. Similarly, \cite{chen_2023_frugalgpt} in the conclusion feels forced as a tag-on.
- **Algorithm 1 is in a verbatim block** rather than a proper algorithmic environment (algorithm2e or algorithmicx). This looks unprofessional in a venue submission.

## Consensus Summary

The paper identifies a genuinely novel optimization axis (topology routing vs. model routing) and formalizes it with a clean, if simple, cost algebra. The writing quality is strong and the positioning against 25+ related systems is thorough. However, the submission has several blocking defects:

1. **Backbone inconsistency** (GPT-4o vs Claude Opus 4.6) undermines all quantitative claims
2. **Figure placeholder** renders as literal text in the PDF
3. **Missing baseline results** (FrugalGPT-adapted, BAMAS-adapted) in Table 1
4. **22-task test set** is too small for the claims made
5. **Pareto knee inconsistency** (tau=0.85 vs sweet spot 0.5-0.7)
6. **3 broken cross-references** rendering as "??" in PDF
